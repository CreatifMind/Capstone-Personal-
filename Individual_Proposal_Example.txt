Title: YOLOv8-seg-Based Automated Pothole Detection with Roboflow Annotation 
 
Abstract 
This project addresses the challenges faced by Malaysia’s  Jabatan Kerja Raya (JKR)  in 
maintaining road safety, caused by the manual inspection and verification of potholes, which leads 
to high workloads, inconsistencies, and stress for employees. The objective is to automate pothole 
detection and severity assessment using the  YOLOv8-seg segmentation model , aimed at 
improving efficiency and consistency. The scope includes  data collection, training the model  on 
annotated pothole images, and  validating its performance using metrics such 
as mAP, Precision, Recall, IoU, and  Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) . By automating the 
verification process, this project aims to reduce the manual workload, improve severity assessment 
consistency, and enhance operational efficiency for JKR. The results of this project are expected 
to streamline pothole detection , contributing to  faster road repairs  and a  healthier work 
environment for JKR personnel, with potential for broader application in other regions facing 
similar challenges. 
 
 
Keywords: 
Pothole detection; Machine learning; YOLOv8-seg; Segmentation model; Ground truth data; 
Image annotation; Real-time pothole detection; Severity assessment; Automation; Road safety; 
Model evaluation

1.0 Introduction 
1.1 Context and Significance 
Malaysia’s Public Works Department (JKR) plays a vital role in sustaining national mobility and 
safety through continuous road maintenance. Yet, despite initiatives such as the MyJalan KKR 
mobile application that allow citizens to report road damage directly, the inspection and 
verification process remains largely manual . Each submission must still be visually examined, 
verified, and categorized by JKR officers before scheduling repairs. This repetitive, high-pressure 
workflow has gradually created a toxic working environment , where officers face mounting 
workloads, inconsistent reporting formats, and unrealistic response deadlines (Rasee et al., 2025; 
Bukhari & Aman, 2023). JKR’s “Zero Pothole” pledge, which mandates that temporary repairs be 
completed within 24 hours and permanent ones within three days, further intensifies the pressure 
to act quickly under data uncertainty (Wong, 2020). Moreover, as citizen participation increases 
through MyJalan, so too does the volume of unstandardized image submissions, many of which 
contain unclear or duplicated information (Rasee et al., 2025). Officers must interpret these images 
manually, often w ithout uniform measurement criteria, leading to fatigue, human err or, and 
inconsistent severity classification—a hallmark of the existing toxic environment. 
 
To mitigate these issues, machine learning (ML)  offers an objective and scalable solution. By 
employing a segmentation -based model such as YOLOv8-seg, potholes can be automatically 
detected and quantified using geometric features extracted from road images. This project 
strengthens model reliability through a ground-truth dataset  collected with direct physical 
measurements, allowing calibration of image -based estimates into actual diameters and areas. 
Automating this early verification stage relieves JKR personnel from tedious screening tasks, 
thereby reducing operational stress and improving the consistency of severity assessments. 
 
Research Questions: 
RQ1: How can machine -learning techniques be trained to automatically detect and segment 
potholes from uncontrolled road images? 
RQ2: How can ground -truth measurements be used to calibrate the model for reliable severity 
estimation? 
RQ3: To what extent can an automated ML framework reduce JKR officers’ manual workload 
and improve verification consistency? 
 
Research Objevtives: 
RO1: To design and implement a YOLOv8-seg model for pothole segmentation under real-world 
imaging conditions. 
RO2: To construct a ground -truth dataset containing physical measurements for severity 
calibration. 
RO3: To evaluate model performance and its potential to reduce manual verification tasks within 
JKR’s workflow.

1.2 Scope, Assumptions, and Constraints 
Scope 
This proposal is primarily focused on the machine learning component of the PaveX system, which 
will automate the process of pothole detection and severity assessment. The scope of the project 
includes data preparation, model training, and model validation using YOLOv8-seg, a state -of-
the-art segmentation model. Specifically, the proposal outlines the development of the YOLOv8-
seg model to segment potholes in road images, including the use of a ground-truth dataset for 
calibration. This dataset will consist of real -world pothole images, accompanied by physical 
measurements (such as diameter, depth, and area) for accurate severity estimation. By integrating 
machine learning  into the pothole detection process, the project aims to relieve the manual 
verification burden on JKR personnel, ultimately improving workflow efficiency and accuracy in 
pothole assessments. 
Assumptions 
1. Training Data: The training images will be sourced from publicly available datasets 
(e.g., Roboflow) and locally collected pothole images from Malaysia. 
2. Ground Truth Accuracy: The ground-truth measurements (e.g., area, diameter, and depth) 
for model calibration are assumed to be accurate, enabling the conversion of image -based 
estimates into real-world units. 
3. Image Quality and Capture Conditions: The images for both training and validation are 
assumed to be captured using smartphones with varying quality and angles. Variations in 
quality and angle will be addressed through data augmentation techniques. 
 
Constraints 
1. Limited Annotated Datasets: Limited access to annotated Malaysian pothole datasets 
may restrict model generalization and require fine-tuning for local pothole characteristics. 
2. Depth Estimation : Depth estimation will rely on geometric approximation  instead of 
specialized sensors (e.g., LiDAR), which may lack precision compared to advanced 
technologies. 
3. Time and Hardware Limitations: Project duration and hardware constraints may limit 
extensive hyperparameter tuning and experimentation.

2.0 Related work 
Recent studies have increasingly adopted YOLOv8-seg for pothole detection  and road hazard 
classification due to its ability to provide pixel-level segmentation for accurate identification. Wu 
et al. (2024) improved YOLOv8-seg for instance segmentation of tea garden roads, using MSDA-
CBAM and DR-Neck to enhance model efficiency and facilitate edge deployment for real-time 
processing. Similarly, Khare et al. (2023) applied YOLOv8 for detecting potholes, sewer covers, 
and manholes, emphasizing the role of supervised learning and Roboflow for data annotation to 
create a robust dataset for real-time detection of road hazards. 
In their work, Yusuf et al. (2025) evaluated the effect of image augmentation techniques such as 
rotation, flipping, and brightness adjustments on improving YOLO models for pothole detection, 
demonstrating that augmentation significantly enhances model robustness  and generalization 
across various conditions. Nurcahyo et al. (2024)  extended the application of YOLOv8 to real-
time pothole detection  and localization, emphasizing the importance of data augmentation and 
supervised learning for creating high-quality datasets. 
The study by Elgamily et al. (2025) applied YOLOv8 for detecting road hazards in remote sensing 
images, utilizing supervised learning  and Roboflow for dataset annotation. It demonstrated the 
model's ability to detect potholes across varied environmental conditions, although real-time edge 
deployment remains an area requiring further. Perumal and Ramasamy (2024)  focused on 
YOLOv8 for real-time pothole detection  in autonomous vehicles , highlighting the use of 
supervised learning and Roboflow for data collection and model training to improve detection 
accuracy and processing speed in dynamic environments. 
Lastly, Wijaya et al. (2024)  explored the use of YOLOv8 for pothole detection in road images 
using open datasets, highlighting the role of Roboflow for data annotation and demonstrating how 
supervised learning can improve detection performance.

While the studies reviewed in this chapter have demonstrated promising advancements in YOLO-

Unlike current models, PaveX addresses gaps in dataset diversity  and real-time deployment ,

The first dataset is sourced from Roboflow, which provides 720 annotated pothole images. These 
images will serve as the training base for the machine -learning model. The annotations in this 
dataset include segmentation masks, which are essential for training the YOLOv8-seg model. Due 
to the relatively small size of this dataset, data augmentation techniques will be applied during the 
data preparation phase to increase its variability and improve model generalization.  
(https://universe.roboflow.com/pothole-segmentation-jfsph/pothole-segmentation-

The second dataset consists of pothole images collected manually in Malaysia using smartphone 
cameras (primarily iPhones). This dataset serves as the testing and ground -truth reference data. 
Approximately 500 images will be gathered, representing real-world citizen-uploaded submissions. 
The images will be taken under varied environmental conditions (e.g., different lighting, weather,

will provide a ground-truth reference for model calibration. This dataset is essential for calculating 
the pixel-to-centimeter constant (K), which will be used to estimate real -world area and depth 
during severity assessment. 
The combined locally collected and ground -truth datasets ensure that the model is tested on 
Malaysian road conditions and calibrated using physically measured pothole dimensions. This 
multi-source approach enhances the model's ability to generalize across different environments 
and maintain real-world applicability.

3.3.2 Data Preparation 
This phase transforms raw pothole images into a format suitable for model input, focusing on 
ensuring image quality, consistency, and diversity for training the YOLOv8-seg model. Since your 
dataset for training is only 720 annotated images, augmentation plays a critical role in expanding 
the dataset and enhancing model generalization. Below is the detailed process: 
a) Data Cleaning 
Objective: 
To verify image integrity and remove low-quality or redundant samples that could reduce 
segmentation precision. 
Process: 
The 720 annotated images downloaded from Roboflow were inspected for sharpness, duplication, 
and label completeness. 
• Blur Detection:  The Variance of Laplacian  method in OpenCV automatically flagged 
images with a variance < 100 as blurred and excluded them (Bansal & Raj, 2015). 
• Duplicate Removal:  Perceptual hashing (pHash)  identified near -duplicate samples by 
comparing Hamming distance between image fingerprints (Swain & Tripathy, 2024). 
• Label Verification: Each mask was visually inspected to ensure consistent boundaries 
between potholes and surrounding road textures. 
This ensured that only high -quality, non-redundant samples were retained for augmentation and 
training. 
 
b) Images Annotation 
 
Objective: 
To ensure accurate segmentation for pothole detection, the Roboflow platform will be used for 
generating polygon-based segmentation masks. These annotations will be verified and refined to 
ensure high quality, enabling the model to generalize effectively when tested on new, unseen data, 
such as pothole images from Malaysia. 
Process Overview: 
1. Roboflow Pre-Annotated Dataset: 
The dataset, consisting of 720 pothole images, has been pre -annotated using Roboflow’s 
auto-annotation tool . This tool generates polygon-based segmentation masks  in the 
YOLOv8-compatible .txt format, which includes class labels and polygon coordinates. 
While Roboflow’s auto-annotation provides initial masks, manual refinement is required 
to ensure the accuracy and precision of the segmentation. (Ultralytics, 2025). 
 
--- PAGE 13 ---

 
2. Augmentation After Annotation: 
After completing the annotation process, augmentation is applied to the dataset. This 
ensures that the segmentation masks remain aligned with the augmented images. As shown 
by Hasanaath et al. (2024) , applying augmentation before annotation may cause 
misalignment between the masks and transformed images , resulting in incorrect 
segmentation. Therefore, we apply augmentation only after the annotation is finalized, 
ensuring that the masks remain accurate throughout transformations like scaling and 
rotation (Yusuf et al., 2025). 
 
3. Manual Verification and Refinement: 
While the initial annotations are auto -generated by Roboflow, manual verification and 
adjustments are necessary to ensure that the segmentation masks accurately reflect pothole 
boundaries. Key tasks in the refinement process include: 
- Polygon Accuracy: Reviewing the polygon coordinates to ensure that the mask vertices 
fully enclose the pothole regions. Any under -segmentation or over -segmentation is 
corrected manually to accurately capture potholes without including irrelevant road 
features, such as cracks or debris (Khare et al., 2023). 
- False Segmentation Handling: If the model detects incorrect boundaries (e.g., detecting 
a pothole in the background or partial occlusion), manual adjustments are made to ensure 
that the mask tightly follows the pothole’s true edges , thus avoiding overfitting to 
incorrect patterns (Wu et al., 2024). 
- Inconsistent Mask Quality : Some masks may suffer from under-segmentation (where 
the mask is too small) or over-segmentation (where irrelevant features are included). 
These are corrected manually to ensure consistency across the dataset, ensuring the model 
learns accurate features. 
4. Annotation Stability and Consistency: 
Once verified, the annotations are exported in the YOLOv8-compatible format. Roboflow 
ensures that the polygon coordinates are scaled consistently , preventing misalignment 
during transformations like scaling or rotation. This guarantees that YOLOv8-seg can 
process high -quality annotations and generalize well on real-world pothole images  
(Ultralytics, 2025; Yusuf et al., 2025). 
 
5. Exporting Annotations: 
After manual verification, the annotations are exported in either YOLOv8-compatible .txt 
or JSON format . This export ensures that each image path corresponds to the correct 
segmentation mask, and the polygon coordinates are consistent across the dataset. These 
verified annotations will be used directly for training the YOLOv8-seg model.

Augmentation Volume and Accuracy Trade-off 
The augmentation multiplier of 5× is chosen as an initial hypothesis based on empirical evidence 
from previous studies, which found 5× augmentation to be optimal for small pothole detection 
datasets: 
• Hasanaath et al. (2024 ) reported a +5.2% mAP gain  with 5× augmentation  on pothole 
datasets of similar size. However, they also noted that augmentation beyond ×5  led to 
overfitting and synthetic noise (FID > 30). 
• Yusuf et al. (2025)  and Rasee et al. (2025)  confirmed that 5× augmentation  improves 
generalization for datasets under 1,000 images, while maintaining FID ≤ 30, ensuring that 
the augmented images closely resemble real-world data. 
• Safyari et al. (2024)  and Wu et al. (2024 ) observed diminishing returns beyond ×6 and 
highlighted potential domain drift that could reduce model performance. 
Therefore, 5× augmentation  will expand the 720 Roboflow images  to approximately 3,600 
augmented images , creating a total dataset size of ~4,320 images . For the combination of 
augmentation will be decided after we run few experiments to ensure it have the best FID.

d) Resizing and Normalization

Process:

• Real-World Dataset (Citizen Uploads): 
For citizen-uploaded images with non-square aspect ratios (e.g., 16:9), the following

1. Resizing: The images will be resized to fit within 640×640 pixels, preserving the 
aspect ratio. Padding will be added to the shorter dimension to create a square 
image. 
2. Normalization: Each resized image will be normalized by scaling pixel values to 
the 0–1 range, ensuring consistency with the Roboflow dataset. Channel-wise 
normalization will also be applied to account for varying lighting conditions and 
camera sensors. 
These steps ensure that both Roboflow and real-world datasets are processed consistently, 
enabling stable training and inference.

3.4.4 Model Evaluation and Validation 
This section outlines the evaluation process for the trained YOLOv8-seg model, focusing on its 
ability to detect potholes, segment them accurately, and validate its performance using real-world 
ground truth data. The model will be assessed using standard segmentation metrics such as Pixel 
Accuracy and Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) , as well as evaluating the area prediction 
accuracy through conversion of predicted areas to real-world units. 
 
a) Model Evaluation Metrics 
Objective: 
To evaluate the model’s ability to detect and segment potholes accurately using standard object 
detection and segmentation metrics. 
Process: 
1. Mean Average Precision (mAP) 
mAP measures the average precision across different IoU (Intersection over Union) 
thresholds. 
o mAP50: Precision at IoU = 50% threshold. 
o mAP50–95: Average precision across multiple IoU thresholds (50–95%). 
2. Precision and Recall 
o Precision: Measures how accurate the model’s predicted potholes are. 
o Recall: Measures how well the model detects all true potholes, including those 
under challenging conditions like occlusion or low visibility. 
3. Intersection over Union (IoU) 
IoU quantifies the overlap between the predicted segmentation mask and the ground truth 
mask. A higher IoU indicates better segmentation accuracy. 
IoU = Area of Overlap
Area of Union  
4. F1-Score 
The F1-Score balances Precision and Recall, offering a single value that reflects both 
detection accuracy and model sensitivity. 
To evaluate the performance of the YOLOv8-seg model, metrics such as mAP50–95, Precision, 
Recall, IoU, and F1-Score will be used. Studies have shown that mAP50–95 effectively evaluates 
segmentation models across various IoU thresholds, while Precision and Recall ensure minimal 
false positives and false negatives, and IoU > 0.5 is considered a reliable measure of segmentation 
quality. Additionally, achieving an overall accuracy greater than 0.85 is often expected for 
--- PAGE 21 ---

 
effective model performance in pothole detection tasks, ensuring high -quality segmentation and 
detection outcomes (Yusuf et al., 2025). 
b) Ground Truth Validation 
Objective: 
To validate the model's predictions by comparing them to real-world ground truth data, ensuring 
that the segmentation masks and predicted areas align with actual pothole measurements. 
Process: 
1. Ground Truth Collection 
100 pothole images  will be manually collected, with real-world measurements  (e.g., 
diameter, area, depth) recorded using tools like tape measures or 3D-scanning apps (e.g., 
Polycam). 
 
2. Area-to-Pixel Conversion 
After testing the model, we will compare the predicted area (in pixels) to the real-world 
area (in cm² or mm²) by using a scaling factor K:

Predicted Area (pixels²)

4.0 Study Implications 
a. How it will help to improve organizational efficiency 
The PaveX project is designed to significantly improve the efficiency of the Jabatan Kerja Raya 
(JKR) by automating key aspects of pothole detection and severity assessment. By implementing 
a machine learning model like YOLOv8-seg, which can automatically detect and segment potholes 
in road images, the process of initial pothole verification becomes streamlined and more accurate. 
The automated model  minimizes the need for manual inspections , reducing the time spent on 
image verification, categorization, and severity classification. 
Through automated pothole detection , the JKR staff  will no longer need to spend long hours 
manually reviewing citizen-submitted images. This will lead to more efficient resource allocation 
and allow officers to focus on more critical tasks, such as repair prioritization and decision-making, 
based on objective severity assessments. Additionally, ground-truth calibration ensures that the 
model’s predictions are consistent and aligned with real -world measurements, leading to more 
reliable and standardized pothole assessments. This improvement in workflow and task automation 
will enhance the overall productivity and responsiveness of the JKR, ultimately contributing to 
faster road repairs and reducing delays in maintenance. 
 
b. How it will minimize the impact of health illness among employees 
The current process of manual pothole inspection and verification places a significant mental and 
physical burden on JKR officers, contributing to a toxic working environment  characterized by 
stress, fatigue, and work-related illness . Officers face high workloads , inconsistent reporting 
formats, and the pressure of meeting tight deadlines, which can lead to burnout and decreased job 
satisfaction (Rasee et al., 2025). Furthermore, this manual process is prone to human error, leading 
to inconsistent assessments of pothole severity and increased stress. 
By automating the early verification stage of pothole detection and severity classification, PaveX 
directly addresses these challenges. With the model performing automated segmentation  and 
severity estimation, JKR staff will experience a reduced workload, leading to less stress and fewer 
opportunities for human error. Officers will also have more time to focus on higher-level decision-
making and quality assurance  tasks. This reduction in manual effort  not only minimizes the 
physical strain associated with repetitive work but also improves mental well-being by removing 
the pressure of meeting unrealistic deadlines and handling inconsistent data. As a result, the PaveX 
system will contribute to a healthier workplace and reduce the risk of work-related health issues.

5.0 Conclusion 
In conclusion, the PaveX system offers a promising solution to the challenges currently faced by 
the Jabatan Kerja Raya (JKR) in pothole detection and road maintenance. By integrating YOLOv8-
seg, a state-of-the-art segmentation model , with ground-truth data  for calibration, PaveX 
automates the process of pothole verification, significantly reducing the workload of JKR staff. 
This leads to more efficient workflows , consistent severity assessments , and ultimately faster 
repair timelines . The automation of the  initial pothole detection  phase will not only improve 
organizational efficiency but will also alleviate the toxic work environment caused by repetitive 
manual inspection tasks, contributing to better employee health and well-being. 
While the PaveX system presents substantial improvements to the pothole detection and 
verification process, it has several limitations. Firstly, the model’s generalization to diverse real-
world road conditions is still dependent on the quality and diversity of the training data. Malaysian 
potholes have unique characteristics, such as varying sizes and shapes, and the model might not 
generalize well if trained only on publicly available datasets or data from other countries. 
Additionally, the accuracy of severity assessments depends on the ground-truth data being accurate 
and representative. If the measurements are not reliable or consistent, the model’s calibration may 
be compromised. Lastly, the depth estimation  for pothole severity still relies on geometric 
approximation rather than more precise methods (such as LiDAR or other sensors), which limits 
the model’s ability to assess the depth of potholes accurately in some cases. 
 
Future Work 
In the future, the PaveX system can be expanded and refined in several key areas: 
1. Improved Dataset Collection : Expanding the ground-truth dataset  to include a larger 
variety of potholes, particularly from different regions of Malaysia, will improve the 
model’s generalization. This can be achieved through crowdsourced data or partnerships 
with local authorities for data collection. 
 
2. Advanced Depth Estimation : The depth estimation component of the system could be 
enhanced by incorporating LiDAR or stereo vision techniques , enabling the model to 
provide more accurate and precise severity assessments based on depth and area. 
 
3. Integration with Real-Time Systems: Future work could involve integrating PaveX with 
real-time monitoring systems , where pothole detection is not only automated but also 
incorporated into repair scheduling  systems. This would ensure that the system can 
--- PAGE 24 ---

 
immediately flag and prioritize potholes based on severity and traffic data, speeding up 
repair workflows. 
 
 
4. Integration with Vehicle Sensors or Drones for Real-Time Monitoring: 
PaveX could be integrated with vehicle-mounted sensors or drone systems to enable real-
time pothole detection and severity assessment as vehicles or drones move across roads. 
For example: 
o Vehicle Sensors: Cars with fixed focal length cameras (e.g., dash cams) could be 
equipped with software running PaveX to detect potholes in real-time as the vehicle 
moves. The camera’s fixed focal length would ensure that potholes at a consistent 
distance from the camera can be accurately detected and categorized without 
distortion. 
o Drone Integration: Drones equipped with high-resolution cameras can cover large 
stretches of road quickly, capturing detailed images of road surfaces. By integrating 
PaveX with drone systems, real-time pothole detection can be achieved, allowing 
for immediate identification  of road hazards over wide areas. This would be 
especially useful for remote or hard-to-reach locations. 
o Fixed Focal Length: Using cameras or sensors with a fixed focal length ensures 
that the model can focus on objects within a specific range, which is crucial for 
detecting potholes with consistent precision. The fixed focal length simplifies the 
problem of image distortion, especially in real-time applications. 
This integration could enable continuous monitoring of road conditions, with potholes detected 
and flagged in real-time, speeding up the process of repair scheduling and road maintenance.
